# Analytical satellite orbit propagation: an exhaustive literature review and novelty assessment

**The paper's core novelty claims are well-supported.** No prior work uses direct residue decomposition in the complex true anomaly exponential F = e^{if} to construct short-period kernels for averaged satellite theory, and no prior work averages in the generalized eccentric longitude K. While the individual mathematical ingredients—the Jacobian dM/df, complex exponential substitution, partial-fraction decomposition, and Hansen coefficients—are classical, their synthesis into a unified algebraic framework for extracting short-period corrections from zonal perturbations appears genuinely new. The closest prior art achieves exact-in-eccentricity results through different mechanisms: finite polynomial Hansen-like coefficients in eccentric anomaly (Lion & Métris, 2013), real-variable closed-form integration via dedicated trigonometric propositions (de Saedeleer, 2005), and Lie-transform normalization in Delaunay or polar-nodal variables (Lara, 2020–2025). This review catalogs every relevant work in the lineage and assesses each against the novelty claims.

## Classical analytical theories established the canonical framework

The modern theory of analytical satellite orbit propagation begins with two landmark papers published in the same 1959 issue of *The Astronomical Journal*. **Brouwer's "Solution of the Problem of Artificial Satellite Theory Without Drag"** (D. Brouwer, *Astron. J.* 64, 378–397, 1959) applied the Von Zeipel method to Delaunay canonical variables (ℓ, g, h, L, G, H), averaging over the mean anomaly ℓ to eliminate short-period terms. The theory treats J₂ at first order for periodic terms and second order for secular terms, with J₃ through J₅ entering at O(J₂²) in Brouwer's ordering. Results are **closed-form in eccentricity**—no power series truncation. Singularities exist at e = 0 (argument of perigee undefined), i = 0 (node undefined), and the **critical inclination** i ≈ 63.4° where a long-period divisor vanishes.

**Kozai** (Y. Kozai, *Astron. J.* 64, 367–377, 1959) solved the same problem via an independent method, using non-canonical Keplerian elements and the Lagrange planetary equations, also averaging over mean anomaly. Results are closed-form in eccentricity. The Kozai approach is computationally more direct but less systematic for higher-order extension. **Lyddane** (R.H. Lyddane, *Astron. J.* 68, 555–558, 1963) removed the e = 0 and i = 0 singularities by reformulating Brouwer's solution in Poincaré canonical variables with components proportional to e cos(ω+Ω), e sin(ω+Ω), sin(i/2) cos Ω, sin(i/2) sin Ω at small e and i, while the critical inclination singularity—an intrinsic dynamical feature—persists. The Brouwer-Lyddane theory remains the foundation of operational analytical propagation, including the SGP4 model used by the U.S. Space Surveillance Network.

The perturbation methodology was transformed by **Deprit's Lie-transform method** (A. Deprit, *Celest. Mech.* 1, 12–30, 1969), introduced as an alternative that remedies the shortcomings of Von Zeipel's mixed-variable generating function approach. Deprit's algorithm produces both direct and inverse canonical transformations explicitly through recursive Poisson bracket chains (the "Deprit triangle"), eliminating the laborious inversion step required by Von Zeipel. **Hori** (G. Hori, *Publ. Astron. Soc. Japan* 18, 287–296, 1966) had independently introduced a Lie series perturbation method three years earlier; Campbell and Jefferys (*Celest. Mech.* 2, 467–473, 1970) established equivalence of the two formulations through explicit calculation to sixth order and a general argument extending to all orders.

**Deprit and Rom** (*Celest. Mech.* 2, 166–206, 1970) carried the main problem to third order using Lie transforms but obtained results as **power series in eccentricity**, which Aksnes (1971) criticized as a regression from Brouwer's closed-form solution. The resolution came through the **elimination of the parallax** in polar-nodal variables (Deprit, *Celest. Mech.* 24, 111–153, 1981), which when followed by Delaunay normalization enabled **Coffey and Deprit** (*J. Guid. Control Dyn.* 5, 366–371, 1982) to obtain the third-order solution in closed form. Healy (*Celest. Mech. Dyn. Astron.* 76, 79–120, 2000) extended this to sixth order symbolically. None of these works use complex variable techniques; all operate with real-variable Poisson brackets and trigonometric identities.

## Modern reformulations push Brouwer's theory to new frontiers

**Martín Lara** has produced the most significant modern reformulations. His "Solution to the Main Problem of the Artificial Satellite by Reverse Normalization" (*Nonlinear Dynamics* 101, 1501–1524, 2020) departs from the traditional parallax → perigee → Delaunay sequence by performing long-period elimination before short-period elimination, revealing a more efficient path to higher orders. "Brouwer's Satellite Solution Redux" (*Celest. Mech. Dyn. Astron.* 133, article 43, 2021) demonstrates that the complete Hamiltonian reduction can be achieved through a **single canonical transformation** in Poincaré's original style, overcoming the Kepler Hamiltonian's degeneracy by adding integration constants to the generating function. His 2024 paper with Fantino, Susanto, and Flores (*Commun. Nonlinear Sci. Numer. Simul.*, 2024) composes the generating functions of multiple transformations into a single mean-to-osculating map, achieving a **33% speedup** in ephemeris evaluation. Most recently, Lara ("Purely Periodic, Second-Order Terms of the J₂-Problem in Closed Form and Arbitrary Variables," *J. Astronaut. Sci.* 72, article 514, 2025) shows that because the perturbation solution derives from a **vectorial generating function**, it can be obtained in arbitrary variables—singular or non-singular, canonical or non-canonical—without recomputation. All of Lara's work uses **Lie-Deprit transforms, Delaunay variables, averaging over mean anomaly**, and **closed-form eccentricity treatment**. No complex variable techniques appear anywhere in his published corpus.

**De Saedeleer** (B. De Saedeleer, *Celest. Mech. Dyn. Astron.* 91, 239–268, 2005) produced the "Complete Zonal Problem of the Artificial Satellite: Generic Compact Analytic First Order in Closed Form." This landmark paper provides a **single analytical formula** valid for any zonal harmonic Jₙ (n ≥ 2) at first order, achieving an asymptotic computational gain of factor 3n/2 for the nth zonal. The result is exact in eccentricity with no series truncation. The method uses **Lie transforms in Delaunay variables**, with averaging over mean anomaly but integration performed via true anomaly using dedicated trigonometric propositions derived by De Saedeleer himself (Gradshteyn & Ryzhik is cited only for identities and cross-checks, not as the primary integration tool). De Saedeleer does **not** use complex variable, residue, or partial-fraction techniques; the closed-form integration relies on original real-variable trigonometric propositions. Note: De Saedeleer does not use the tangent-half-angle substitution t = tan(f/2). This is the closest prior work in terms of *scope* (generic zonal, closed-form) but differs fundamentally in *technique*.

**Mahajan and Alfriend** (B. Mahajan, K.T. Alfriend, *Celest. Mech. Dyn. Astron.* 131, article 45, 2019; correction *CMDA* 133, 25, 2021) address the case where any arbitrary spherical harmonic (not just J₂) is the dominant perturbation—relevant for lunar orbiters and irregular bodies. They developed a complementary approach (explicitly stating their expressions "are derived differently from" De Saedeleer's) and develop a new long-period transformation. The method uses Lie-Deprit transforms, Delaunay variables, closed-form eccentricity treatment, and no complex variable methods.

## The DSST lineage defines semi-analytical propagation

The **Draper Semi-Analytical Satellite Theory** (DSST), initiated by Cefola at MIT/Draper Laboratory, is the most comprehensive operational semi-analytical propagator. Broucke and Cefola (*Celest. Mech.* 5, 303–310, 1972) established the equinoctial orbit elements (a, h, k, p, q, λ) as the foundation—non-singular at e = 0 and i = 0. McClain's "Blue Book" (CSC/TR-77/6010, 1977) formalized the **Generalized Method of Averaging** (GMA), and the definitive reference is Danielson, Neta, and Early's technical report (NPS-MA-95-002, 1995).

DSST averages over the **mean longitude λ** (the fast variable in equinoctial elements). Conservative perturbations are averaged analytically; non-conservative forces use Gaussian numerical quadrature. Short-period corrections are expressed as **Fourier series in mean longitude with coefficients involving Hansen coefficients**, and these series are **truncated at some order in eccentricity**. This truncation is the theory's Achilles heel: for e > 0.3, errors in J₂² terms become noticeable; for e > 0.7 (GTO/Molniya orbits), position errors can reach hundreds of meters.

**San-Juan, López, and Cefola** (*J. Astronaut. Sci.* 69, 1292–1318, 2022) addressed this with a **second-order closed-form J₂ model** consistent with DSST, using an extension of the Lie-Deprit method in Delaunay variables. Their averaging uses the differential relation a²η dℓ = r² df (conservation of angular momentum) to convert mean-anomaly integrals to true-anomaly integrals, achieving exact-in-eccentricity results. This is the most directly comparable prior work in terms of addressing DSST's eccentricity limitation, though it uses Lie transforms and real-variable integration rather than residue decomposition.

Earlier attempts at resolving the eccentricity problem include Folcik and Cefola (AMOS Conference, 2012), who used Gauss-Kronrod quadrature—accurate but requiring 465 evaluation points at e = 0.75. A 2024 paper in *Acta Astronautica* (S0094576524004727) further improved the J₂² computation by introducing new partial derivative forms that eliminate difficulties with infinite Fourier series multiplication.

The Orekit open-source library implements both DSST (since release 7.0) and Brouwer-Lyddane propagators. DSST in Orekit supports geopotential through 50×50, third-body, drag, and SRP, validated against the legacy Fortran DSST Standalone across 29 test cases. Known limitations include expensive tesseral short-periodic derivatives and retention of eccentricity-truncated models as defaults.

**SGP4/SDP4** represents the simplified operational end of the spectrum. Derived from Brouwer's theory with modifications by Lane, Hoots, and Roehrich (Spacetrack Report #3, 1980), SGP4 treats only J₂ with empirical drag (B*), achieving approximately 1 km accuracy at epoch, degrading rapidly thereafter (Vallado 2006, attributing accuracy figures to Hartman 1993). Vallado et al. (AIAA-2006-6753, 2006) produced the standard modern reference implementation. Other semi-analytical systems include **STELA** (CNES, for end-of-life compliance), **DRAMA/OSCAR** (ESA debris mitigation), **STORM** (Airbus, originally for Mars), and **PPT2** (Naval Space Command, Brouwer-based with DSST tesseral terms).

## Generalized Equinoctial Elements redefine the element-set landscape

Classical equinoctial elements (Broucke & Cefola, 1972; Walker, Ireland & Owens, *Celest. Mech.* 36, 409–419, 1985) resolve the e = 0 and i = 0 singularities through the projections h = e sin(ω+Ω), k = e cos(ω+Ω), p = tan(i/2) sin Ω, q = tan(i/2) cos Ω. They remain singular for retrograde equatorial orbits (i = π), addressable via a retrograde set. Hintz (*J. Guid. Control Dyn.* 31, 785–790, 2008) surveyed 22+ element sets and their trade-offs.

**Giulio Baù's research lineage** provides the intellectual foundation for GEqOE. Beginning with DromoP elements (Baù, Bombardelli & Peláez, *Celest. Mech. Dyn. Astron.* 116, 53–78, 2013), which embed the disturbing potential into a generalized angular momentum definition under a second-order Sundman transformation, the work progressed through EDromo (Baù et al., *Mon. Not. R. Astron. Soc.* 454, 2890–2908, 2015)—non-singular, 8-element—and intermediate elements (Baù & Roa, *Celest. Mech. Dyn. Astron.* 132, 10, 2020)—universal across all energy values using Stumpff functions—to the culminating **GEqOE paper** (Baù, Hernando-Ayuso & Bombardelli, *Celest. Mech. Dyn. Astron.* 133, 50, 2021).

GEqOE consists of six elements: the generalized mean motion ν (absorbs total energy including disturbing potential U), generalized eccentricity projections p₁ = g sin Ψ and p₂ = g cos Ψ (where g is the generalized eccentricity), generalized mean longitude L, and inclination parameters q₁, q₂ identical to classical equinoctial. The **generalized eccentric longitude** K = G + Ψ satisfies a generalized Kepler equation L = K + p₁ cos K − p₂ sin K, with the Jacobian dL/dK = 1 − p₁ sin K − p₂ cos K = r/a. When U = 0, all quantities reduce exactly to classical equinoctial elements. The non-osculating ellipse defined by GEqOE varies more slowly than the osculating ellipse, producing "remarkable improvement" in propagation accuracy under J₂.

The progression J₂EqOE (Aristoff, Horwood & Alfriend, *Celest. Mech. Dyn. Astron.* 133, article 9, 2021) → GEqOE → M-GEqOE (Gupta et al., 2025, for cislunar dynamics) represents an increasingly sophisticated strategy of absorbing perturbative nonlinearities into element definitions. Hernando-Ayuso et al. (*J. Guid. Control Dyn.* 46, 654–669, 2023) demonstrated that GEqOE maintains uncertainty realism throughout day-long propagations, outperforming all other representations in Cramér-von Mises tests.

**Critically, no prior work has used GEqOE (or any generalized element set with intermediate anomaly) for orbit averaging in the perturbation-theory sense.** All applications of GEqOE to date are for special perturbations (numerical integration) and uncertainty propagation, not general perturbation theory.

## Complex variable methods have not penetrated orbit averaging

This is the most critical section for novelty assessment. After exhaustive searching across ADS, Google Scholar, arXiv, Springer, and ScienceDirect, **no prior work was found that uses residue calculus or partial-fraction decomposition in F = e^{if} to derive short-period corrections in satellite theory.**

**Hansen coefficients** are the closest mathematical relatives. Defined as X_s^{n,m}(e) such that (r/a)^n e^{imf} = Σ_s X_s^{n,m}(e) e^{isM}, they encode the eccentricity dependence of Fourier decomposition in mean anomaly. Their definition is inherently a contour integral when z = e^{iM} is introduced. Extensive computational literature exists—Giacaglia (*Celest. Mech.* 14, 515–523, 1976), Hughes (1981), Breiter, Métris & Vokrouhlický (*Celest. Mech. Dyn. Astron.*, 2004)—but all treat Hansen coefficients as eccentricity functions computed via recurrence relations or hypergeometric series, never via residue extraction in the F-plane.

**Sadov** (S.Yu. Sadov, *Celest. Mech. Dyn. Astron.* 100, 287–300, 2008) is the closest prior art, explicitly studying "poles and residues" of Hansen coefficients—but in the **complex η = √(1−e²) plane**, analyzing their behavior as functions of the eccentricity parameter. This is fundamentally different from working in the complex F = e^{if} plane.

**Lion and Métris** (G. Lion, G. Métris, *Adv. Space Res.* 51, 1–9, 2013; also Lion, Métris & Deleflie, arXiv:1605.07901, 2016) developed Hansen-like coefficients Z_s^{n,m}(e) with respect to the **eccentric anomaly** E rather than mean anomaly. For the third-body case (0 ≤ |m| ≤ n, n > 0), these reduce to **finite polynomials** in the auxiliary parameter b = e/(1+√(1−e²))—the Fourier series terminates, requiring no truncation. This achieves exact-in-eccentricity results through a different mathematical mechanism: the finite polynomial nature arises from the terminating trigonometric relationships between (r, f) and E. Lion et al. (2016) used these to construct analytical theory for highly elliptical orbits with third-body perturbations.

**The tangent-half-angle substitution** t = tan(f/2), widely used in closed-form orbit averaging, is mathematically related to F = e^{if} via t = −i(F−1)/(F+1). Both convert trigonometric integrands to rational functions. However, no work in the celestial mechanics literature explicitly connects the tangent-half-angle approach to contour integration or residue methods. Lara (2020–2025) uses this substitution (or equivalent real-variable techniques) purely algebraically. Note: De Saedeleer (2005) does not use the tangent-half-angle substitution.

**Jefferys** (W.H. Jefferys, *Celest. Mech.* 3, 390–394, 1971) observed (as "well known," without proof) that the equation of the center f − ℓ cannot be integrated in closed form within the algebra of trigonometric functions. The residue approach in F circumvents this limitation by working in the algebra of **Laurent series** rather than trigonometric functions, a genuinely different algebraic framework.

**Tisserand's** *Traité de Mécanique Céleste* (1889–1896) and **Brumberg's** *Analytical Techniques of Celestial Mechanics* (Springer, 1995) are the two most comprehensive classical references. Neither appears to employ complex contour integration for orbit averaging, though Brumberg studies Hansen coefficients as analytic functions and develops algorithmic "Keplerian processors" for closed-form manipulations.

The specific claim about the **fixed pole structure** of dM/dF—poles at F = −q and F = −1/q, independent of zonal degree—is mathematically straightforward: since dM/df = η³/(1+e cos f)² and 1 + e cos f = e(F+q)(F+1/q)/(2F) when F = e^{if}, these poles are determined solely by the two-body geometry, not by the perturbation. While this factorization is implicit in classical theory, **no prior work was found that explicitly identifies it, notes its zonal-degree independence, or exploits it for short-period kernel construction**.

## Averaging methods span canonical and non-canonical traditions

Lie-transform perturbation theory (Deprit 1969, Hori 1966) is the standard for canonical (Hamiltonian) systems. The homological equation {H₀, Wₙ} + Kₙ = Φₙ separates the averaged Hamiltonian Kₙ (kernel of the Lie derivative operator) from the generating function Wₙ (requiring inversion on the image). Short-period corrections follow from the generating function through Poisson bracket chains.

**Non-canonical averaging** has an equally established tradition. Hori himself extended his method to non-canonical systems (*Publ. Astron. Soc. Japan* 23, 567–587, 1971), replacing the Poisson bracket with Lie derivatives of vector fields. The DSST's Generalized Method of Averaging (McClain, 1977) is explicitly non-canonical, applying directly to variation-of-parameters equations for equinoctial elements. Ahmed and Tapley proved equivalence between Hori's non-canonical method and KBM (Krylov-Bogoliubov-Mitropolsky) averaging. The GEqOE averaged theory, being based on variation-of-parameters equations rather than a Hamiltonian, falls squarely in this non-canonical tradition.

**The choice of averaging variable matters profoundly.** Standard practice averages over mean anomaly M or mean longitude λ (Brouwer, DSST, Lara). True anomaly f is used as an intermediate integration variable by many (de Saedeleer 2005, San-Juan et al. 2022). Eccentric anomaly E has been used by Kaufman and Dasenbrock (*J. Spacecraft Rockets* 10, 377–383, 1973) and systematically by Lion and Métris (2013). **No prior work averages in the generalized eccentric longitude K** (or any intermediate anomaly between M and f defined through a generalized Kepler equation). The paper's change of independent variable to K, combined with conversion to F = e^{if}, is without precedent.

At first order, canonical and non-canonical averaging produce identical mean element rates—the averaged equations of motion are the same regardless of method. Differences emerge at second order and in the form of short-period corrections. Lara's 2025 result (*J. Astronaut. Sci.* 72, 514) that exact separation of purely short-period effects requires a **non-canonical transformation** via a "vectorial generator" is particularly relevant: it suggests that the paper's non-canonical approach may have inherent advantages for short-period correction purity.

**Superconvergent methods** (Kolmogorov's technique, achieving quadratic convergence ε → ε² → ε⁴ → ...) exist theoretically (Cary, DOE/ET/70074, 1978; Howland, *Celest. Mech.* 15, 327–352, 1977) but are not used in operational satellite theory.

## Short-period corrections remain a frontier of competing approaches

The state of the art for short-period corrections divides into three camps:

- **Fourier series truncated in eccentricity**: The original DSST approach. Hansen coefficients encode eccentricity dependence as power series. Computationally straightforward via recurrence but breaks down at high eccentricity. The Laplace limit (e ≈ 0.6627) provides a theoretical convergence bound for mean-anomaly Lagrange series.

- **Closed-form real-variable approaches**: Brouwer (1959) was closed-form from the start. De Saedeleer (2005) extended this to generic zonals. Lara (2020–2025) achieves closed-form results at second order through Lie transforms in Delaunay/polar-nodal variables, with the 2024 composed-transformation paper offering 33%+ speedup. San-Juan et al. (2022) provide closed-form J₂² for DSST. All use real-variable algebraic manipulation.

- **Finite polynomial expansions in eccentric anomaly**: Lion and Métris (2013) showed that Hansen-like coefficients in E reduce to finite polynomials in the auxiliary parameter b for the third-body case (0 ≤ |m| ≤ n, n > 0), yielding exact results without series truncation.

The paper under review opens a **fourth camp**: extracting short-period corrections as closed-form expressions in the eccentricity parameter q through partial-fraction decomposition of Laurent polynomials in F—purely rational for the vast majority of harmonics (those with zero secular rate), and rational plus a smooth logarithmic correction for the few harmonics carrying secular rates. This is algebraically distinct from all three existing approaches. The closed-form expressions require no series truncation, no integral-table lookup, and no recurrence relation—the short-period part is obtained by algebraic decomposition, with the pole structure of dM/dF providing a universal template independent of zonal degree.

## SSA applications and symbolic compilation set the applied context

**Ana Sánchez Rivero** (Universidad de Sevilla, predoctoral FPU researcher) develops geometric conjunction filters for space traffic management. Her SDC9 paper (Rivero, Vázquez & Merz, 2025, paper 167) introduces enhanced Space Occupancy (SO) and Space Occupancy Path (SOP) filters incorporating zonal harmonic secular rates and King-Hele drag theory, validated on Starlink. A companion paper (Rivero, Baù, Vázquez & Bombardelli, 2025, SDC9 paper 168) computes minimum distance between RSO trajectories analytically using Cook's linear secular theory extended to higher-order eccentricity effects. Her work on conjunction filtering based on minimum distance between perturbed trajectories is published in *Communications in Nonlinear Science and Numerical Simulation* (2025, 109236, doi:10.1016/j.cnsns.2025.109236). While operationally important, this work uses analytical propagation results as inputs to filters rather than developing new propagation theory.

Mean-element-based uncertainty propagation connects directly to averaged theory. Aristoff, Horwood, and Alfriend (*Celest. Mech. Dyn. Astron.* 133, article 9, 2021) showed that their J₂ equinoctial elements (J2EqOE, a distinct element set from GEqOE) preserve uncertainty realism far longer than Cartesian or standard equinoctial elements. Hernando-Ayuso et al. (*JGCD* 46, 654–669, 2023) subsequently demonstrated that GEqOE outperforms all predecessors in this regard. An averaged GEqOE theory would provide the analytical mean-to-osculating map needed to fully exploit this advantage.

**heyoka.py** (Biscani & Izzo, *Mon. Not. R. Astron. Soc.* 504, 2614–2628, 2021) is directly relevant to the paper's implementation strategy. Its `cfunc` facility JIT-compiles symbolic expressions to LLVM-optimized, SIMD-vectorized native machine code, with `diff_tensors` providing arbitrary-order symbolic differentiation. heyoka already implements SGP4 as a fully differentiable compiled analytical propagator (∂SGP4), demonstrating the paradigm. **No prior work was found that compiles symbolic short-period correction expressions from an averaged perturbation theory to native machine code via heyoka's cfunc**, making this aspect of the paper novel in implementation if not in concept.

Other recent developments include **automatic differentiation** via DACE (Differential Algebra Computational Engine) for jet transport uncertainty propagation (Massari, Di Lizia & Rasotto, *J. Aerosp. Inf. Syst.* 14, 493–503, 2017), **hybrid ML-SGP4** approaches (San-Juan et al., *Acta Astronaut.* 137, 2017), **Koopman operator methods** (Arnas & Linares, *JGCD* 44, 1909–1923, 2021), and a fully analytical lunar satellite propagator using Hamiltonian normal forms (Efthymiopoulos et al., arXiv:2505.09241, 2025). None employ complex-variable residue techniques.

## Definitive novelty assessment across all four claims

**Claim 1: No prior use of direct residue/partial-fraction decomposition in F = e^{if} for short-period kernels.** *Verdict: Supported.* Across 60+ years of analytical satellite theory—from Brouwer (1959) through Lara (2025)—no work performs orbit averaging via residue extraction in the complex true anomaly exponential. Hansen coefficients implicitly involve contour integrals when z = e^{iM} is introduced, but this connection has never been explicitly exploited for short-period kernel construction. Sadov (2008) studies poles and residues of Hansen coefficients in the complex eccentricity parameter plane, not the anomaly plane. The tangent-half-angle substitution is mathematically related but used purely algebraically.

**Claim 2: No prior averaging in generalized eccentric longitude K.** *Verdict: Supported.* Standard averaging uses M or λ (Brouwer, DSST, Lara), with f as intermediate integration variable (de Saedeleer, San-Juan). Eccentric anomaly E has been used as integration variable (Kaufman & Dasenbrock 1972, Lion & Métris 2013) but not a *generalized* eccentric longitude defined through a perturbed Kepler equation. The GEqOE K is a non-Keplerian angle that absorbs perturbative effects; its use for averaging is unprecedented.

**Claim 3: Closed-form expressions in q and Q, exact in eccentricity.** *Verdict: Partially novel.* Many prior works achieve exact-in-eccentricity results: Brouwer (1959), de Saedeleer (2005), Lara (2020–2025), San-Juan et al. (2022), and Lion & Métris (2013) all avoid eccentricity truncation. However, the specific form—**closed-form expressions in the eccentricity parameter q arising from partial-fraction decomposition** (purely rational for zero-mean harmonics, rational plus a smooth logarithmic correction for secular-rate harmonics)—appears novel. Existing closed-form results involve η = √(1−e²), trigonometric combinations, or polynomial eccentricity functions, not rational/logarithmic expressions in q obtained algebraically from pole residues.

**Claim 4: Fixed pole structure of dM/dF (poles at F = −q and F = −1/q, independent of zonal degree) not previously exploited.** *Verdict: Supported.* The factorization 1 + e cos f = e(F+q)(F+1/q)/(2F) with F = e^{if} is a standard complex-analysis result, and the Jacobian dM/df = η³/(1+e cos f)² is universally known. However, no prior work explicitly identifies these poles in the F-plane, observes their independence from the perturbation (zonal degree), or exploits them as a universal algebraic template for short-period extraction. This observation—that all zonal averaging integrals share the same denominator structure—is the key conceptual insight enabling the unified partial-fraction approach.

## Conclusion

This literature review spans **seven decades of analytical and semi-analytical satellite theory**, from the classical Von Zeipel/Brouwer/Kozai foundations through the Lie-transform revolution (Deprit, Hori), the semi-analytical operational framework (DSST/Cefola), modern closed-form reformulations (de Saedeleer, Lara, San-Juan), generalized element sets (Baù), and contemporary applications in space situational awareness and compiled symbolic computation.

The paper's four novelty claims withstand scrutiny. The synthesis of F = e^{if} complex exponential representation, the fixed dM/dF pole structure, partial-fraction decomposition, and averaging in generalized eccentric longitude K constitutes a genuinely new mathematical framework for constructing short-period corrections. It sidesteps the limitation observed by Jefferys (1971) by working in Laurent series rather than trigonometric function algebra, and it unifies the treatment of arbitrary zonal harmonics through the degree-independent pole structure—an advantage shared conceptually with de Saedeleer's (2005) generic formula but achieved through an entirely different mechanism.

The closest prior works are de Saedeleer (2005) in scope, Lion and Métris (2013) in achieving exact-in-eccentricity results via non-standard anomaly variables, San-Juan et al. (2022) in addressing DSST's eccentricity limitation with closed-form methods, and Lara (2025) in exploring non-canonical transformations for exact short-period separation. None employ complex-variable residue techniques. The compilation of symbolic expressions to machine code via heyoka's cfunc represents a novel implementation pathway. A minor caveat: Tisserand's *Traité* (1889–1896) and Brumberg's *Analytical Techniques* (1995) could not be fully electronically searched, though given the specificity of the technique, undetected prior art in these sources is unlikely.
