Analytic orbit theory with any arbitrary spherical harmonic
as the dominant perturbation

Bharat Mahajan1           · Kyle T. Alfriend1

Received: 1 April 2019 / Revised: 24 August 2019 / Accepted: 28 August 2019
© Springer Nature B.V. 2019



Abstract
In the gravitational potential of Earth, the oblateness term is the dominant perturbation, with
its coefficient at least three orders of magnitude greater than that of any other zonal or tesseral
spherical harmonic. Therefore, analytic orbit theories (or satellite theories) are developed
using the Keplerian Hamiltonian as the unperturbed solution, oblateness term as the first-order
and the remaining spherical harmonics as the second-order perturbations. These orbit theories
are generally constructed by applying multiple near-identity canonical transformations to
the perturbed Hamiltonian in conjunction with averaging to obtain a secular Hamiltonian,
from which the short-period and long-period terms are removed. If the oblateness term is
the only first-order perturbation, then the long-period terms appear only in the second- or
higher-order terms of the single-averaged Hamiltonian, from which the short-period terms
are removed. These second-order long-period terms are separated from the Hamiltonian
by the first-order generating function using a second canonical transformation. This results
in a secular Hamiltonian dependent only on the momenta. However, in the case of other
gravitational bodies with more deformed shapes compared to Earth such as moons and
asteroids, the oblateness coefficient may have the same order of magnitude as some of the
higher spherical harmonic coefficients. If these higher harmonics are treated as the first-order
perturbation along with the oblateness term, then the long-period terms appear in the first-
order single-averaged Hamiltonian. These first-order long-period terms cannot be separated
using the generating function in the conventional way. This problem occurs because the
zeroth-order Hamiltonian, i.e., the Keplerian part, is degenerate in the angular momentum.
In this paper, a new approach to the long-period transformation is proposed to resolve this
issue and obtain a fully analytic orbit theory when for the perturbing gravitational body,
any arbitrary zonal or tesseral harmonic is the dominant perturbation. The proposed theory
is closed form in the eccentricity as well. It is applied to predict the motion of artificial
satellites for the two test cases: a lunar orbiter and a satellite of 433 Eros asteroid. The
prediction accuracy is validated against the numerical propagation using a force model with
6 × 6 gravity field.

1 Introduction

The beginning of the space age generated numerous analytic orbit theories for estimating
the orbits of space objects (Mahajan 2018). Brouwer (1959) used von Zeipel’s method to
obtain a perturbation solution to the artificial satellite problem including the zonal harmonics
from J2 to J5 of the geopotential. His theory includes the secular terms up to second order
and short-period and long-period terms up to first order. In contrast, Vinti (1959) obtained
a closed-form solution using the spheroidal coordinates for the motion of an object about
an oblate spheroid. Garfinkel (1959) also obtained a closed-form solution of a modified
geopotential that included a portion of the oblateness potential. Kozai (1959) developed a
similar theory to that of Brouwer by separating the secular, short-period, and long-period
terms from the disturbing potential and then computed the variations of the orbital elements
using Lagrange’s planetary equations. Of these, Brouwer’s theory with the addition of the
atmospheric drag effects emerged as the basis for orbit prediction in the USA and maintaining
the space object catalog.
    von Zeipel’s method of performing canonical transformations suffer from a drawback of
producing mixed-term generating functions. First Hori (1966) and then Deprit (1969) pro-
posed the use of near-identity transformations for constructing the canonical transformations,
which avoids the limitations of von Zeipel’s method. Subsequently, many special canonical
transformations (Deprit 1981a; Alfriend and Coffey 1984; Coffey and Alfriend 1981) were
proposed to transform the Hamiltonian in a simplified form suitable for further normaliza-
tion. Using these transformations, the orbit theories for the artificial satellites of Earth have
been constructed up to third and higher orders with the oblateness term considered as the
first-order perturbation (Deprit 1981b; Coffey and Deprit 1982; Kudryavtsev 1995; Kinoshita
1978).
    In the geopotential, the oblateness coefficient (J2 ) is of O (10−3 ) and all the other coef-
ficients have magnitudes of O (J22 ) or smaller. Therefore, the perturbation solutions are
generally developed in powers of J2 with the oblateness term as the first-order perturbation
and the rest of the harmonics treated at the second order. When using the canonical perturba-
tion methods, the short-period averaging of the original Hamiltonian produces long-period
terms, which are dependent on the argument of periapsis, in the new averaged Hamiltonian.
These long-period terms occur for all the spherical harmonics included in the original Hamil-
tonian except for the oblateness term. As a result, these long-period terms first appear at the
second order and because the Keplerian Hamiltonian is degenerate in the angular momen-
tum, these second-order terms are eliminated from the single-averaged Hamiltonian using
the first-order term of the long-period generating function.
    However, a problem occurs when any of the higher harmonic coefficients are of O (J2 )
and must be treated as first-order perturbations. Then, the corresponding long-period terms
appear at the first order in the single-averaged Hamiltonian and they cannot be separated from
it using a long-period transformation in the conventional way. This is due to the fact that the
zeroth-order (Keplerian) Hamiltonian is degenerate in the angular momentum, and thus, no
partials of the generating function with respect to the argument of periapsis are available to
eliminate the first-order long-period terms. As a result, the long-period transformation does
not result in a Hamiltonian that is a function of only the momenta; consequently, one does

not obtain a set of mean elements having only secular rates. In this paper, a novel approach
is developed for addressing this problem and obtaining a secular Hamiltonian resulting in
coordinates that are completely secular or in other words vary linearly with time. The proposed
approach can be implemented using a two-step averaging process. The first step is the standard
averaging process for the short-period terms for an arbitrary zonal or tesseral harmonic using
the Keplerian Hamiltonian as the zeroth-order Hamiltonian, carried out to the desired order.
In this work, the short-period averaging is, in fact, performed separately for the zonal and
tesseral harmonics. The result is a new Hamiltonian that does not depend on the true or mean
anomaly. Then, in the second step, the secular portion of this Hamiltonian with contributions
from all the spherical harmonics considered is chosen as the new zeroth-order Hamiltonian.
The remaining long-period part of the Hamiltonian is treated as the perturbation. This new
zeroth-order non-Keplerian Hamiltonian is non-degenerate in the angular momentum, and
the long-period averaging process can now be used successfully to separate the long-period
terms.
   The following sections present the complete mathematical development of a first-order
orbit theory using a complete gravitational potential with all the zonal, sectorial, and tesseral
harmonics considered as the dominant perturbations. The short-period terms due to the sec-
torial and tesseral harmonics are eliminated first from the Hamiltonian in Sect. 3. This
is followed by another transformation for separating the zonal short-period terms due to
the zonal harmonics. In Sect. 4, the long-period terms are separated from the averaged
Hamiltonian obtained in the previous section in order to completely normalize the original
Hamiltonian. The formulae for the averaged Hamiltonians and the corresponding generating
functions are derived in a generalized form, which makes them valid for any arbitrary spher-
ical harmonic. Section 5 applies the proposed theory to the motion of a lunar orbiter and a
satellite of 433 Eros asteroid, followed by conclusions in Sect. 6.

3 Short-period averaging

For a first-order theory, there is no coupling among any of the zonal, sectorial, and tesseral
harmonics. As a result, the complete Delaunay normalization of the Hamiltonian H as given
in Eq. (14) can be performed using the three separate transformations. First, the short-period
terms due to zonals and tesserals are separated from H using two transformations, and
then, the long-period terms are separated using the long-period transformation to obtain a
completely secular Hamiltonian. In this section, the two transformations for separating all
the short-period terms from H are derived.

5 Applications

The analytic orbit theory developed in the previous sections is applied to the motion of two
artificial satellites: the first is a lunar orbiter, and the second orbits the asteroid 433 Eros.
The unnormalized spherical harmonic coefficients of the Moon1 and 433 Eros2 are given in
Tables 1 and 2. Inspection of the Moon’s coefficients shows that C20 has the largest magnitude
followed by C31 , C22 , and C60 . The latter three are of the same order of magnitude and only
one order of magnitude smaller than the former. In fact, the ratio of the magnitudes of C31
to C20 is 0.14. For 433 Eros, C22 , S22 , C40 , and C60 are one order of magnitude smaller
than the oblateness coefficient. The ratios of C40 to C20 and C22 to C20 are 0.33 and 0.45,
respectively. In the case of Earth, C20 is at least three orders of magnitude larger than any
other coefficient and the largest ratio of the magnitudes of any coefficient to that of C20 is
only 0.002. Therefore, an orbit theory for 433 Eros must include C22 and C40 along with
C20 as the first-order perturbations. It is noted that for a complete theory for a lunar orbiter,
the third-body perturbation due to Earth has significant effects and needs to be included (De
Saedeleer 2006). However, in the present work, the analytic theory for the lunar orbiter only
considers the gravitational perturbations and the third-body effects are ignored, which can
be incorporated using the conventional approaches as shown by De Saedeleer (2006).
    The analytical expressions as derived for the first-order secular Hamiltonian and gener-
ating functions in the previous sections are all expressed in a generalized form valid for any
arbitrary spherical harmonic. As a result, a first-order orbit theory can be easily built using
these generalized expressions with spherical harmonics included up to any degree and order.
In the present work, these generalized expressions are implemented in a symbolic algebra
software package and their partial derivatives are computed symbolically for zonal harmon-
ics from J2 to J6 in order to compute the expressions for the secular rates, short-period,
and long-period variations of the Delaunay elements. The expressions for the short-period
variations due to the sectorial and tesseral harmonics are derived by hand. For demonstration,
these analytical expressions are used to construct two orbit theories: the first for the lunar
orbiter and the second for the 433 Eros orbiter, with all the spherical harmonics up to 6 × 6
considered dominant perturbations. As discussed earlier, the long-period or resonant effects
of the sectorial and tesseral harmonics are not considered in the present work.

6 Conclusions

In general, the short-period averaging of the zonal spherical harmonics with degree greater
than two results in secular and long-period terms. If these spherical harmonics are treated as
second-order perturbations with the oblateness term as the first-order perturbation, then the
second-order long-period terms can be removed by averaging with respect to the argument of
perigee for constructing satellite theories of Earth. However, for bodies with more deformed
shapes, it may be necessary to consider the higher spherical harmonics as the dominant
perturbation. The conventional approach to separating these long-period terms from the
Hamiltonian fails. A novel method to carry out the long-period transformations is developed,

harmonic. Results presented for the motion of a lunar orbiter and a 433 Eros orbiter in a
6 × 6 gravity field, show that the proposed first-order analytic theory is in agreement with
the results obtained from the numerical integration.
