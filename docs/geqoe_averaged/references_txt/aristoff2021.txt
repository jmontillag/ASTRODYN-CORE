Abstract
This paper defines a set of six non-singular orbital elements designed specifically for the char-
acterization of uncertainty in the state of a resident space object in circular or elliptic orbit and
demonstrates their use for uncertainty propagation in the context of the perturbed two-body
problem of orbital mechanics. As evidenced by the time evolution of the Cramér–von Mises
test statistic, representation of the orbital state probability density function in J2 EqOE yields
less nonlinear uncertainty propagation and provides covariance and uncertainty realism for
much longer periods of time than what is possible using Cartesian coordinates or even
equinoctial orbital elements, without an appreciable increase in computational cost.

Keywords Satellites · Coordinate systems · Uncertainty propagation

1 Introduction
Space situational awareness (SSA) is the comprehensive knowledge of the near-Earth space
environment accomplished through the tracking and identification of orbiting space objects
needed to protect man-made satellites and maintain awareness of potential collision hazards
and on-orbit threats. Uncertainty realism is the proper characterization of the probability
density function (PDF) of the state of a system.1 Uncertainty (and covariance) realism in
1 Covariance realism is the proper characterization of the covariance in the state of a system under Gaussian
assumptions. It implies that the estimate of the mean is the true mean (i.e., the estimate is unbiased) and that the
covariance possesses the right size, shape, and orientation (i.e., consistency). Uncertainty realism relaxes the
Gaussian assumptions. Covariance realism is a necessary but not sufficient condition for achieving uncertainty
realism. An extensive study on covariance and uncertainty realism in the SSA domain is provided in the report
of Poore et al. (2016).

the orbital state of a space object is a common requirement for many SSA functions, espe-
cially ones involving uncertainty propagation such as space surveillance tracking, conjunction
assessment, sensor tasking, and maneuver detection (Aristoff et al. 2015b). Achieving uncer-
tainty realism in the current or future state of a space object is challenging due to long time
gaps between measurement updates, nonlinear and non-conservative dynamics, and long
propagation times. One proven approach to improving covariance and uncertainty realism
during uncertainty propagation is through the use of orbital-element-based coordinate sys-
tems that absorb the dominant nonlinearity in the equations of motion: the 1/r 2 term in the
geopotential. Of particular interest are non-singular orbital elements that prevent the singu-
larities that exist for the standard orbital elements at zero eccentricity and zero inclination,
such as the equinoctial orbital elements (Broucke and Cefola 1972). One might ask if one
can do better; that is, can one absorb additional nonlinearities in the equations of motion to
further improve uncertainty realism, while at the same time respecting constraints pertaining
to implementation and runtime costs? The answer is a resounding yes.
    This paper defines a set of orbital elements, coined the J 2 equinoctial orbital elements
(J2 EqOE), designed specifically for the characterization of uncertainty in the state of a space
object in circular or elliptic orbit, and demonstrates their use for uncertainty propagation
in the context of the perturbed two-body problem of orbital mechanics. What distinguishes
the J2 EqOE from existing sets of orbital elements is the combination of the following. First,
the J2 EqOE are non-singular for eccentricities less than one and inclinations less than 180◦ .
Hence, complications do not arise when propagating the uncertainty of nearly circular orbits
or nearly equatorial orbits.2 Second, the J2 EqOE account for the dominant perturbation to
Earth-orbiting satellites, the J2 zonal perturbation that arises from the oblateness of the Earth
(Montenbruck and Gill 2005; Vallado 2013). Thus, when used for uncertainty propagation, the
J2 EqOE enable one to maintain covariance and uncertainty realism for much longer periods
of time (Horwood et al. 2014a, b). Third, the J2 EqOE are straightforward to implement.
Fourth, the J2 EqOE are a set of only six non-singular orbital elements. Hence, unlike non-
singular sets consisting of seven or more orbital elements, they do not require additional orbits
to be propagated (e.g., when using sigma-point- or particle-based methods for uncertainty
propagation) nor higher-dimensional state transition matrices to be numerically integrated
(e.g., when using Kalman-based methods for uncertainty propagation). Thus, use of the
J2 EqOE does not appreciably increase the computational cost of uncertainty propagation.
    We note that the J2 EqOE can be described as a particular set of “mean” orbital elements.
However, use of the J2 EqOE is not restricted to a particular force model nor orbital propagator.
Thus, we prefer to view the J2 EqOE as simply a coordinate system in which the dynamics
are less nonlinear, much like the osculating equinoctial elements (EqOE), but with the added
benefit of having absorbed the J2 perturbation. We also note that Gim and Alfriend (2005)
developed a similar first-order J2 analytic orbit theory using equinoctial elements and applied
it to the problem of formation flying. We stress that use of the J2 EqOE in a Kalman filter
does not require one to use an analytical propagation theory (e.g., general perturbations) in
the prediction step. Indeed, the uncertainty propagation examples presented in this paper use
numerical integration based on the authors’ Gauss–Legendre implicit Runge–Kutta orbital
propagator (Aristoff et al. 2014a, 2015a) replete with high fidelity force models.

    The primary application of this work is the uncertainty propagation (filter prediction)
problem. We acknowledge that the filter correction step is equally important in many SSA
use cases. However, there are applications where preserving the Gaussian assumption in the
prediction step of a Kalman filter is advantageous (i.e., by representing the Gaussian PDF in an
orbital element coordinate system), most notably, in the problem of estimating the probability
of collision (PC) between two objects. As argued in Aristoff et al. (2014b), it is more efficient
to propagate the states and covariances representing the two objects in (osculating or J2 )
equinoctial orbital elements to the time of closest approach. Then, if needed, the propagated
Gaussian PDFs can be converted to Gaussian mixtures in Cartesian coordinates to facilitate
the PC calculation (which is inherently defined in Cartesian coordinates). This procedure
is computationally less costly than propagating an entire Gaussian mixture in Cartesian
coordinates from epoch to the time of closest approach (since propagating a Gaussian PDF
under the nonlinear dynamics is generally more expensive than converting a Gaussian PDF
from element space to Cartesian space). This paradigm is followed in the KRATOS PC
algorithm (Horwood et al. 2016). An analogous procedure is applicable to nonlinear filtering
problems in SSA. In the implementation of the filter correction step, one can convert the
propagated orbital element Gaussian PDF to a Gaussian mixture in Cartesian coordinates, if
the nonlinearities in the state-to-measurement function are sufficiently high to warrant such a
conversion, otherwise one can apply the conventional (extended or unscented) Kalman filter
update.
    The layout of this paper is the following. In Sect. 2, we define the coordinate transform
between EqOE and J2 EqOE. An algorithm for inverting the transform is also presented.
In Sect. 3, we illustrate the time evolution of the J2 EqOE, and demonstrate the use of the
J2 EqOE for uncertainty propagation using a range of initial conditions. In Sect. 4, we discuss
potential extensions and alternatives to the J2 EqOE for the purposes of improved uncertainty
propagation. Section 5 concludes our study. In the appendix, we provide a numerical example
of the J2 EqOE to EqOE conversion, inclusive of all intermediate steps, to serve as a test case
and debugging tool for readers who may want to implement the transformations.

2.2 J2 IOE to/from IOE

In this section, we define a J2 IOE to IOE transformation by considering only short-period
terms from the Hamiltonian potential equation, expanded in a perturbation series up to order
J2 , following Brouwer (1959) and Lyddane (1963). We seek a definition that is valid for
all eccentricities less than one, and for all inclinations less than 180◦ . Therefore, we do
not include the long-period terms as they possess a singularity at the critical inclinations
of approximately 63.4◦ and 116.6◦ . Fortunately, long-period terms (which are functions of
the “slowly” varying argument of perigee through trigonometric relations) can be safely

2.2.2 IOE to J2 IOE

The transformation from IOE to J2 IOE requires inverting the J2 IOE to IOE transform defined
in Sect. 2.2.1. Specifically, it requires solving six coupled nonlinear equations which map
the J2 IOE to the IOE, similar to how one inverts the transform introduced by Brouwer (Cain
1962). Note that we seek a numerical solution that does not require computation of the
Jacobian of the J2 IOE to IOE transform. A careful implementation of fixed-point iteration
satisfies this criterion.
    Four considerations are necessary for a robust solution of the nonlinear system of equations
using fixed-point iteration. First, we iterate over the scaled J2 IOE to improve the conditioning
of the fixed-point iterations. Second, we treat the angular variables via a branch-cut correction.

Third, we enforce constraints on the J2 IOE. Fourth, we use an initial guess that enables

Once implemented, round-trip tests of the coordinate system are recommended.
Step 1 Input the IOE and let the initial guess for the J2 IOE be these elements. If any of the
IOE are less than 0.01, increase them to 0.01 to improve convergence.
Step 2 While the two-norm of the residuals is greater than the iteration tolerance and the
number of iterations is less than the maximum allowable number of iterations, do Steps
2a–2d.
Step 2a Compute the IOE from the J2 IOE guess using the transformation defined in Sect. 2.2.1
and scale the result by (R, 1, 1, 1, 1, 1), where R is the radius of the Earth.
Step 2b Perform a branch-cut correction on the sixth IOE with respect to the sixth J2 IOE,
and compute the residuals between the (scaled) input J2 IOE and those found in Step 2a.
Step 2c Compute the two-norm of the residuals found in Step 2b. If the norm is lower than
all previously computed residual norms (or it is the first iteration), record the current (scaled)
IOE (i.e., the best solution found thus far).
Step 2d Compute the next guess for the (scaled) IOE by subtracting the residuals from the
current guess. Enforce the constraint that I2 , I3 , I4 , and I5 must be between −1 and 1.
Step 3 Return the J2 IOE corresponding to the lowest residual norm.

3.1 Time evolution of the J2 EqOE

In this section, we validate the coordinate transformations by numerically propagating the
orbits listed in Table 1 and plotting the AEqOE and J2 EqOE states at every 1/1000th of an
orbit for three orbital periods. The purpose of this exercise is to verify that the time variation
of the J2 EqOE is less nonlinear than that of the EqOE and that no biases are observed between
the element sets for a given orbital element, where we have converted the semi-major axis to
mean motion for the sake of comparison. Two different force models are used to represent
truth: (i) a force model consisting of only J2 gravity, and (ii) the “full dynamics” model
that includes the additional perturbations described above. Recall that the J2 EqOE attempt
to “absorb” the J2 perturbation, and that higher-order gravitational perturbations as well
as solar-lunar perturbations and other forces are omitted. The examples wherein we use a
higher-fidelity “truth” to model the dynamics will allow us to quantify the effect of these
omissions, and give us some indication of the potential benefit when using the J2 EqOE for
uncertainty propagation.
    Figure 1 illustrates the coordinate transform for an object in LEO. As expected the J2 EqOE
exhibit less time variation than the AEqOE, and no bias is observed. Similar results are
obtained for both force models, indicating that the J2 term is by far the most dominant
contributor to the perturbations in this example (Scenario 1).
    Figure 2 illustrates the coordinate transform for an object in GEO. Again, the J2 EqOE
tend to dampen the oscillations observed in the orbital elements, albeit less so when the
higher-fidelity force model is used. Here, solar radiation pressure, and to a lesser extent the
higher-order gravitational perturbations, causes the noticeable difference between the left and
right columns; however, the magnitude of the oscillations is much less than what is observed
in Fig. 1 for the low Earth orbit. Note that the oscillation in the J2 EqOE observed in orbital
elements p and q for J2 dynamics is negligible (below machine precision) and arises from
the fact that the inclination is zero in this example (Scenario 2), which also serves to validate
the coordinate transform for satellites with zero eccentricity and/or zero inclination.
    Figure 3 illustrates the coordinate transform for an object in HEO. Near perigee, the
AEqOE exhibit large-amplitude oscillations, especially in the mean motion, that are largely
mitigated by use of the J2 EqOE. This is true for both force models under consideration.
Similar results are obtained for both force models, indicating that the J2 term is also the most
dominant contributor to the perturbations.
    In all three examples provided, use of the J2 EqOE resulted in a time variation of the orbital
elements that was less nonlinear (i.e., more dampened oscillations). This was especially
apparent in the LEO and HEO test cases, and suggests that covariance and uncertainty realism
may be easier to maintain when the orbital state PDF is represented in J2 EqOE as opposed to
AEqOE or EqOE. Additional examples are provided in the next section to demonstrate this
fact.

   of distributions is significantly greater than that of propagating a single distribution and
   then splitting it into a mixture (at the final epoch time).

4 Discussion

Use of the J2 EqOE is not restricted to linearized propagation nor the unscented transform.
As shown in Horwood et al. (2014a), the J2 EqOE can be combined with more sophisticated
methods for uncertainty propagation, for example, the prediction step of the Gauss–von Mises
filter (Horwood and Poore 2014), to further maintain uncertainty realism during uncertainty
propagation. Indeed, for extreme uncertainties, the Gauss–von Mises filter is preferable due
to its rigorous treatment of angular uncertainty and its computational efficiency (i.e., like
the UT, only 2m + 1 sigma points need to be propagated, where m is the length of the state
vector).

9   Page 16 of 19                                                                         J. M. Aristoff et al.


   As demonstrated in Fig. 4, the choice of coordinate system for representing the orbital
state PDF has a profound impact when the covariance is considered “large.” In this LEO
example, the initial standard deviation along the semi-major axis (which coincides roughly
with the radial direction) is 20 km. Such a large uncertainty can arise when creating a PDF
from a single track of data (i.e., a short sequence of sensor observations) through an initial
orbit determination process. Some applications (e.g., conjunction assessment) may require
one to propagate this PDF and achieve covariance realism in order to obtain an accurate
metric (e.g., probability of collision). Representing the PDF in J2 EqOE can significantly
prolong covariance realism during propagation compared to Cartesian coordinates or EqOE.
   Given the large impact of the choice of coordinate system on maintaining covariance real-
ism during uncertainty propagation, it would be of interest to compare—using the statistical
goodness-of-fit test described in Sect. 3.2—other analytic and semi-analytic orbital element
theories such as those developed by Gim and Alfriend (2005) and Neelon et al. (1997),
respectively. However, this is beyond the scope of the present study, and we leave it as future
work.


5 Conclusions

The achievement of covariance and uncertainty realism is paramount to the success of several
SSA functions including space surveillance tracking, conjunction assessment, sensor tasking,
and maneuver detection. Without uncertainty realism, space objects can become lost, colli-
sions can go undetected, and false alarms can lead to costly maneuvers. The J2 equinoctial
orbital elements, developed in this paper, are an example of a non-singular coordinate system
in which the dynamics are less nonlinear and in which the orbital state PDF can be faithfully
propagated for longer periods of time without a significant increase in computational cost. As
demonstrated above using realistic force models and initial conditions, use of the J2 EqOE is
especially beneficial when propagating objects in LEO and HEO, where the J2 perturbation is
most pronounced. Specifically, uncertainty realism can be maintained up to 28 times longer
by using the J2 EqOE in place of Cartesian coordinates, and up to 14 times longer by using
the J2 EqOE in place of the (Broucke and Cefola 1972) osculating EqOE.
