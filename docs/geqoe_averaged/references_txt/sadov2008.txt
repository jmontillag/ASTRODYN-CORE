Abstract Hansen’s coefficients in the theory of elliptic motion with eccentricity e are
studied as functions of the parameter η = (1 − e2 )1/2 . Their analytic behavior in the complex
η plane is described and some symmetry relations are derived. In particular, for every Hansen
coefficient, multiplication by suitable powers of e and η results in an entire analytic function
of η. Consequently, Hansen’s coefficients can be in principle computed by means of rapidly
convergent series in powers of η. A representation of Hansen’s coefficients in terms of two
entire functions of e2 follows.

Keywords Two-body problem · Elliptic motion · Hansen coefficients ·
High eccentricity · Series expansion · Analytic functions · Poles and residues

1 Introduction

This paper deals with properties of Hansen’s √ coefficients in the theory of elliptic motion
considered as functions of the parameter η = 1 − e2 . Their expansions in powers of the
eccentricity e have long been in use; comprehensive tables were compiled by Cayley in
1861 and later extended (Deprit and Rom 1967; Jarnagin et al. 1965). Further analytical
developments and efficient computational techniques for expansions of various functions
of elliptic motion have continued to appear, motivated by applications of such expansions
in perturbation calculations (Brumberg and Fukushima 1994; Hughes 1981; Klioner et al.
1998). While classical expansions were primarily designed to handle the most important
astronomical case of small eccentricities, more recent efforts have targeted the entire range
0 ≤ e < 1 (Brumberg and Brumberg 1999; Klioner et al. 1998).

    We approach the problem of high eccentricities from the other end. The origin of our
expansions, η = 0, corresponds to e = 1, a parabolic orbit.
    As it turns out, the Hansen coefficients as functions of η possess nice analytic proper-
ties. As Theorem 2 (Sect. 7) asserts, every Hansen coefficient can be split into product of
a simple explicit factor and an entire analytic function of η of at most exponential growth.
Consequently, the second factor is represented by a series which converges uniformly in any
bounded domain, in particular, in the interval η ∈ [0, 1] corresponding to e ∈ [0, 1]. More-
over, the convergence is factorially fast. If desired, one can dispense with η and return to the

    My interest to expansions centered at η = 0 comes from the study of the equation of
the attitude motion of a satellite (Beletsky 1965; Chernous’ko 1964; Lutze and Abbit 1969)
in the mathematically challenging limiting case e → 1− . While such a limit problem may
be perceived as a purely analytical exercise, it provides an insight into classification of
generalized periodic motions and their stability in the moderate, realistic eccentricity range
(Bruno and Varin 2004). Higher order perturbation theory for the limit problem (Sadov 1997,
2008) requires a systematic study of expansions in η of a subfamily of Hansen’s coefficients.
Observations made in Sadov (2006) are generalized here.

6 Local behavior of Hansen coefficients at η = 0, ±1

Recall the notion of the order of an analytic function at a point (Ahlfors 1966, §4.3.3). If a
function g(η) is analytic in a punctured neighborhood of η0 ∈ C, then the order of g at η0 is
the integer (if it exists)
                  ordη0 g = max{N ∈ Z : g(η) = O(η − η0 ) N as η → η0 }.
The order is negative at poles and positive at zeros of the function. If η0 is an essential
singular point for g, then by definition ordη0 g = −∞. The function g is meromorphic if
ordη g > −∞ for all η ∈ C.
   The notion of order directly extends to functions with algebraic branch points, like
(η − η0 )1/2 . The order at a branch point will be fractional.

9 Conclusion

The main results of the paper are stated in three theorems. Theorem 1 asserts that Hansen’s
                                                                                        √
coefficient X kn,m with even m − k is a meromorphic function of the variable η = 1 − e2
with possible poles only at η = 0, ±1. Lower estimates for orders (of poles or zeros) at these
special points are obtained; the result for η = 1 is consistent with the classical D’Alembert
characteristic of the Maclaurin series of X kn,m in powers of e (Remark 2 in Sect. 7). If m − k
is odd, the above statements hold true for the function (1 − η2 )1/2 X kn,m (η). Factoring away
the poles yields an entire function of η with exponential growth estimates (Theorem 2). The
entire function of η can be split into the odd and even parts and thus expressed in terms of
two entire functions of e2 (Theorem 3).
    On a technical level, a residue decomposition of the contour integral that defines the
nontrivial factor in the multiplicative decomposition of X kn,m is studied (Sect. 3). A more
general family of functions depending on four parameters, from which Hansen’s coefficients
are derived by specialization, is considered. Two involutive transformations of variables
(Sect. 4) are employed to derive useful relations between the members of the family.
    The qualitative results stated in Theorems 2 and 3 provide, in particular, for a possibility
to construct rapidly convergent series in any of the two parameters, η or e, for evaluation of
X kn,m uniformly on the interval [0, 1]. The word “rapidly” refers to the rate of decay of Taylor
coefficients when n, m, and k are fixed. We did not attempt to address practical computational
issues arising due to non-uniformness of the estimate (42) in n, m, and k. Also, algorithmic
aspects of evaluation of the coefficients are beyond the scope of this paper.
