Abstract. When the perturbation affecting a Keplerian motion is proportional to r-"(n 2 3), a canonical
transformation of Lie type will convert the system into one in which the perturbation is proportional to
r *. Because it removes parallactic factors, the transformation is called the elimination of the parallax.
In the main problem for the theory of artificial satellites, the elimination of the parallax has been con-
ducted by computer to order 4. The first order in the reduced system may now be integrated in closed
form, thereby revealing the fundamental propertyofthe first-order intermediary orbits in line with Newton’s
Propositio XLIV.
Extension beyond order | leads to identify a new class of intermediaries for the main problem in nodal
coordinates, namely the radial intermediaries.
The technique of smoothing a perturbation prior to normalizing the perturbed Keplerian system,
of which the elimination of the parallax is an instance, is applied to derive the intermediaries in nodal
coordinates proposed by Sterne, Garfinkel, Cid—Palacios and Aksnes, and to find the canonical diffeo-
morphisms which relate them to one another and to the radial intermediaries.
1. Introduction
So much having been published about the main problem in the theory of an artificial
satellite, it may be taken as axiomatic that a claim of originality may (and probably
will) be met by a statement of the opposite. With all this wealth, empty spaces never-
theless exist where an author can contribute more than an inadvertent duplication
or a personal derivation of results already in print. We believe the present communica-
tion falls into one of the cracks. The elimination of the parallax is a technique by
which a Keplerian system in a central field of force in r’"(n 2 3) is converted into
a quasi-Keplerian system with a variable angular momentum. To our knowledge,
this technique is new to perturbation theories in non-linear and celestial mechanics.
In the theory of artificial satellites, the equations of motion are generally solved
analytically in two steps. A canonical transformation eliminates the short period
terms depending on the mean anomaly. Then the averaged equations are solved
either by numerical integration, as in the semi-analytical theories of Lidov and Cefola
(1974) or Wu Lian-Da et al. (1978), or by analytical normalization, a second canonical
transformation being performed to remove the long-period terms in the argument
of perigee and the longitude of the ascending node. Both methods tend generally
to underestimate the amount of work required in the course of averaging over the
mean anomaly. Yet the practical difficulties met in computing average elements
from initial conditions and in evaluating multivariate Fourier series have discouraged
engineers from programming analytical solutions for predicting and improving

Undeniably the method imposes the conventional algorithms to start with yet
another canonical transformation. However, the procedure amounts to factoring
the elimination of the short-period terms into the product of two transformations,
each one definitely simpler than the product. The elimination of the parallax removes
those short-period variations due to a torsion exercised on the apsidal frame by the
perturbations in r_” (n = 3) and in the argument of latitude. Left in the system are
the short-period variations resulting from irregularities in the equation of the centre.
The factoring is reminiscent of the parceling of terms made in lunar theory by de
Ponteécoulant.
Computer programs are in the making to eliminate the parallax in the theory
of artificial satellites; they are built by stepwise refinements, each extension closely
collating its results with developments obtained by various authors. How else could
one guard the computer programs from slipping into error? While this sustained
vigilance stimulates to assimilate anterior contributions into the present scheme,
it may (and will) trap some readers into commenting that the ‘last word has been
said long ago in the theory of artificial satellites’. To the contrary, we believe that,
Stripped of its non-essential complications, the main problem is now amenable to
further investigations into its characteristic structures.
The computational complexities induced by the short-period fluctuations have
been paid a great deal of attention since they were uncovered by Kozai (1962) while
he extended to order 2 the first-order solution developed by Brouwer (1959). One
way of mitigating the difficulty consists in breaking the main problem in two parts,
a dominant term made of a separable Hamiltonian called the intermediary and a
remainder to be regarded as a perturbation affecting the intermediary (Sterne, 1960).

Now that all known intermediaries in polar variables have been shown to share
the capital property of being natural intermediaries to the main problem in the
theory of artificial satellites, is there a reason to favour one rather than the others”?
By the ‘criteria of merit’ proposed by Garfinkel and Aksnes (1970), the radial inter-
mediaries rate higher than the zonal ones since the separation of variables in their
Hamilton—Jacobi equation does not involve elliptic integral for the argument of
latitude. By the same criteria, among the radial intermediaries, the Hamiltonian
R, is especially commendable for ‘1. its accuracy, 2. its simplicity, and 3. the ease
of calculating higher order perturbations’.
In Table III, the natural intermediaries in Whittaker variables for the main problem
have been ordered in a genealogical tree by increasing complexity of their components
V, and V,. By duality, the tree constitutes an ordering by increasing simplicity in
the generators and the equations of their naturalizing canonical transformations.
Which intermediary one picks on the tree is a matter of personal taste, commanded
to a large extent by the software context. After all, no one said it with more glee
than Prolix (Goscinny and Uderzo, 1972): Simplicity is difficult to program, but
recognizable when it occurs.
9. The Radial Intermediary

problem by adopting the Keplerian principal part M, as the intermediary, thus
viewing the orbit as a kind of envelope of a family of ellipses in continuing deforma-
tion. As was indicated in Section 6, an averaging over the mean anomaly will then
reproduce the Hamiltonian in the perigee elements (g,G) obtained by Brouwer
and Kozai. In a subsequent averaging over the argument of perigee, the Poincare-
Lindstedt method breaks down formally at the singular inclinations I, mod z=
tan” ' 2; away from these singularities, it produces first-order long period variations
in g.

At any given moment, the satellite and its phantom are at the same distance from
the centre of the Earth and they have the same radial velocity. Intuitively speaking,
the torsion has adjusted the parameters of the phantom’s Keplerian motion to
make superfluous the addition of short-period variations to the radial variables
(r, R) of the satellite; for instance, the mean motion of the phantom is determined
not by the principal part M, but by the total energy R,. The phantom’s perigee is
fixed; the satellite’s perigees form a polygonal contour ona fixed circle in the orbital
plane. The torsion in @ translates in Brouwer’s model into a uniform rotation of
the perigee ; the mean motion of perigee for Brouwer’s orbit equals the rate of increase
in the average of 0 over the anomalistic period of the phantom.
So far, in contrast to the approximate solution restricted to bounded orbits as it
was built by Brouwer, we have produced an exact solution for the radial inter-
mediary valid everywhere in the phase space except on the submanifold

10. Conclusion
A case has been made for canonical transformations aimed not at generating integrals
of a dynamical system but only at removing non-essential intricacies. Applied to the
main problem in satellite theory, the elimination of the parallax has put the system
in a state where the developments have become elementary at the second order and
manageable without the help of a computer at the third order. Conceptually, it has
offered a new perspective along which one can regroup various theories based on the
notion of an intermediary and increase their effectiveness. Finally, the elimination
of the parallax itself has produced a new class of intermediaries. It is not unreasonable
to expect that the radial intermediary will contribute one day to our understanding of
the phase flow in the main problem for the theory of artificial satellites.
