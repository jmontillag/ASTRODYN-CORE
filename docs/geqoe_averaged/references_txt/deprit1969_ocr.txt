CANONICAL TRANSFORMATIONS
DEPENDING ON A SMALL PARAMETER
ANDRE DEPRIT
Boeing Scientific Research Laboratories, Seattle, Wash., U.S.A.
(Received 20 September, 1968)
Abstract. The concept of a Lie series is enlarged to encompass the cases where the generating function
itself depends explicitly on the small parameter. Lie transforms define naturally a class of canonical
mappings in the form of power series in the small parameter. The formalism generates nonconservative
as well as conservative transformations. Perturbation theories based on it offer three substantial
advantages: they yield the transformation of state variables in an explicit form; in a function of the
original variables, substitution of the new variables consists simply of an iterative procedure involving
only explicit chains of Poisson brackets; the inverse transformation can be built the same way.

From a practical viewpoint, inversions and substitutions are cumbersome opera-

they do not enter into the details of the operations. As a result, their theory is left

For instance, ephemerides of an artificial satellite based on a theory elaborated by

elements averaged over an anomalistic period are not explicitly developed in terms

We felt the shortcomings of von Zeipel’s method when we attempted to apply it
to normalize a Hamiltonian system in the neighborhood of an equilibrium (Deprit,
1966). As a result of this failure, we found a way of constructing the transformation
(1) in a straightforward recursion based on an extension to quasi-periodic solutions
of Poincaré’s continuation of periodic orbits (Deprit and Henrard, 1967). However,
we still failed to give a good answer to the problem of converting in the normalizing
variables a function of the primitive state variables (x, X).
In the present communication, we offer
a Theory of Transformations that, in our
opinion, accomplishes everything that von Zeipel’s method does in a general perturba-
tion theory, and yet remedies its shortcomings and inconveniences.

For the sake of clarity in the exposition, we would like to present the basic idea as
a generalization of the concept of an infinitesimal canonical transformation. We
proceed in two stages. First we review howa Lie series defines a canonical mapping
as a formal power series of a small parameter ¢, provided the generating function
itself does not depend on e. Then we overcome this restriction by introducing the
concept of a Lie transform, and we show how they naturally define the canonical
transformations contemplated by von Zeipel’s method. We complete this paper by
outlining the basic steps of a general theory of perturbations based on Lie transforms.
A little consideration of the past literature makes it apparent that the class of
canonical transformations introduced in this communication has been envisioned by
several authors.
(a) It naturally encompasses the transition mappings from initial states at ft, to the
states at a nearby instant t as they are determined by the general solution of a con-
servative Hamiltonian system. Grdbner (1960) recommends presenting the transition
mappings as Lie series generated by the Hamiltonian itself; recently Stumpff (1968)
commented on possible applications of Grébner’s formalism. In that context, Section
3 of this paper offers a generalization of Lie series to nonconservative Hamiltonian
systems (see also Leimanis, 1965).
(b) Von Zeipel’s technique and the present one are identical to the first order.
At this approximation, the conversion rule applied to a function that does not depend
explicitly on the small parameter has been proposed by Iszak (1963). As a matter of
fact, Iszak’s formula was inspired by a remark of Dziobek (1888) that purports to
justify a similar operation in the first order of a planetary theory.
(c) The construction that we propose here ought to be discussed in relation with
an algorithm already proposed by Hori (1966, 1967). Although the preamble to his
construction seems amenable to a serious objection, a thorough comparison of the
|
two formalisms could prove very informative.
(d) The present formalism justifies the way Schechter (1967) corrected a normaliza-
tion around an equilibrium carried by Breakwell and Pringle (1966) in the case of a
resonance. What Schechter does there amounts to deriving from a von Zeipel’s
generating function in mixed variables the Lie transforms that define the transforma-
tion in explicit form.
(e) Most recently, Lacina (1969) has proposed a recursive algorithm to construct
explicitly a canonical transformation that renders all variables, except
a moment,
ignorable in a conservative Hamiltonian function. Lacina’s mapping is not derived
from a generating function.

5. Conclusions
Canonical mappings defined by Lie transforms as formal power series of a small
parameter constitute the natural ingredient of Transformation Theory applied to
Hamiltonian systems.
The formalism we just described is now being developed in four areas: a revision
of Brouwer’s theory of an artificial satellite, its extension to Lunar Orbiters, Birkhoff’s
normalization of the triangular centers of libration in the reduced problem of three
bodies, and the analysis of resonances.
