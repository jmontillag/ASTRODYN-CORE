Purely Periodic, Second‑Order Terms of the J2‑Problem
in Closed Form and Arbitrary Variables

Martin Lara1

Accepted: 27 June 2025 / Published online: 22 July 2025
© The Author(s) 2025



Abstract
For the J2-problem, the mean-to-osculating transformation that guarantees the exact
separation of long- and short-period terms up to the second order of J2 has been
recently reported by the author. The transformation was computed in Delaunay
canonical variables. However, because such kind of solution is derived from a vec-
torial generating function, it can be obtained in arbitrary variables—either singular
or not, canonical or non-canonical—without need of recomputing the perturbation
solution. The procedure is illustrated for the insightful set of semi-equinoctial varia-
bles. It could be equally applied, if desired, to compute a second-order, closed-form,
analytical model truly consistent with the Draper semianalytical satellite theory.

Keywords Perturbed Kepler motion · Semianalytical methods · Mean elements · Lie
transforms · Noncanonical perturbation theory · Hamiltonian perturbations


1 Introduction

In the theory of artificial satellites, mean variations in strict agreement with the
average behavior of osculating elements are routinely obtained with the generalized
method of averaging [1–3]. To that aim the original, osculating, variation equations
of the perturbation problem are formulated in a given set of variables, which are
customarily chosen nonsingular. The equations of the mean-element rates, as well as
the short-period corrections that provide the transformation from mean elements to
osculating elements, are then constructed in the same set of variables according to
the perturbation method. The mean elements are efficiently integrated with numeri-
cal methods, whereas the osculating elements are recovered from them using ana-
lytical corrections. This semianalytic integration scheme based on the decomposi-
tion of the motion into long- and short-period effects gives rise to very efficient orbit

propagation tools. Among them, the Draper semianalytical satellite theory (DSST in
what follows) stands out due to its sustained development for the last fifty years [4,
5].
    Still, the generalized method of averaging is not the sole option for materializing
the semianalytical framework. The same results can be obtained with the method of
Lie transforms [6–8], which in addition enjoys greater versatility. Indeed, the fact
that the short-period corrections are derived from a generating function, which is of
vectorial character in the more general approach, makes to construct the semiana-
lytical solution in different ways possible when using the method of Lie transforms.
    Such versatility of the method of Lie transforms is certainly useful in the decom-
position of the motion into the mean-to-osculating transformation and the long-
period variations. The exact separation of the perturbed dynamics into short- and
long-period terms is not really a constraint of the semianalytic approach, although it
may be required in specific applications dealing only with the mean-element dynam-
ics. On the contrary, since mean elements can be defined in a variety of ways, the
decomposition of the perturbation solution into the mean-element dynamics and
the mean-to-osculating transformation may adopt different formulations [9]. But an
extra advantage of the method of Lie transforms stems from the fact that, once the
generating function has been obtained in a desired set of variables, the mean-to-
osculating transformation can be reformulated in a different set of variables without
need of recomputing the whole perturbation theory from scratch [6, 8].
    That the possibility of reformulating a perturbation solution in a different set of
variables, instead of its complete rederivation, is a definitive advantage is clearly
recognized when the mean-to-osculating transformation is approached in closed
form—a common wish in artificial satellite theory due to the variety of satellite
missions involving highly eccentric orbits. Indeed, because usual perturbations of
the Keplerian motion do not depend explicitly on the mean anomaly, but through
its implicit dependence on the true or eccentric anomalies, the analytical computa-
tion of the mean-to-osculating transformation in closed form confronts the solution
of nontrivial integrals beyond first-order effects. Rules for solving the majority of
these integrals are based on the geometric relations of the ellipse as well as on the
dynamics of the Kepler problem. Therefore, it looks wise, as well as more expedite,
to rely on previous, well-tested results in the literature based on the latter [10–17].
In spite of the fact that different sets of variables may be better suited for dealing
with particular perturbations of the orbital motion than the traditional set of Keple-
rian elements [18], approaching from the beginning the closed-form solution of the
required definite and indefinite integrals in a different set of variables could compli-
cate unnecessarily the closed-form computation of the perturbation theory.
    Of particular interest in different astrodynamical applications is the solution of
the J2-problem. Because it deals with the dominant gravitational perturbation of
most circumterrestrial orbits, it was traditionally known as the main problem of arti-
ficial satellite theory [19]. Different perturbation theories succeeded in the compu-
tation of high-order effects of J2 in closed form [16, 20, 21]. However, these kinds
of solutions usually proceeded by partial Hamiltonian normalization to achieve the
total Hamiltonian reduction, in which case the strict splitting of terms of different
nature is irrelevant. Moreover, even if the exact separation were desired, achieving
The Journal of the Astronautical Sciences (2025) 72:35                    Page 3 of 32 35


the correct partitioning could not be possible due to the Hamiltonian nature of the
employed methods [22]. A final, noncanonical tuning is needed in that case in order
to obtain the exact isolation of the short-period terms that are purely periodic in the
mean anomaly [23].
    Therefore, applications looking for the exact separation of the mean variations
beyond the first order of J2 seem to be doomed to relying on noncanonical pertur-
bation methods. When such kind of solution is approached with the generalized
method of averaging, one remains tied to computing the mean-to-osculating trans-
formation in the same variables in which the mean variations are evaluated.
    The example provided by DSST of the inconvenience one finds in this process
when the closed form is desired is quite illustrative. The difficulties seemed unsur-
mountable at the beginning, leading DSST developers to the alternative search of
approximate solutions based on expansions of the elliptic motion [24]. Publication
of an analytic procedure for computing the mean variations in closed-form was
delayed by about twenty years [25]. Yet, the implementation of the closed-form
solution for the mean variations was not reported until fourteen additional years
[26]. Refer to the latter reference for more detailed descriptions. Still, the proposed
closed-form formulation of the mean variations was found not optimum due to the
fact that it needed numerical quadrature for its evaluation, and the highly-desired
analytical formulation remained open. Recent patches provided both the mean-to-
osculating transformation and the mean variations in closed form without resorting
to numerical quadrature [27]. However, the purely periodic nature of the mean-to-
osculating transformation of such solution is not guaranteed for the use of Ham-
iltonian methods in its derivation. This fact led subsequent attempts in modeling
the second-order of J2 terms to give it up with the closed form. Instead, the efforts
were concentrated in improving the evaluation of analytical short-period corrections
based on series expansions [28].
    The wanted closed-form theory providing the exact separation of short- and long-
period terms up to the second order of J2 was finally announced in [29] (see, also,
[30]). The approach followed there relied on the noncanonical version of the method
of Lie transforms [7, 8] and the use of Delaunay canonical variables. The latter nota-
bly facilitated the closed-form solution of the quadratures and indefinite integrals
emerging in the Lie transforms procedure. On the other hand, because the third
component of the angular momentum is one of the Delaunay variables, the perturba-
tion solution to the J2-problem is additionally simplified in view of this variable is
an integral of the osculating dynamics. However, as it happens with the Keplerian
elements, the Delaunay variables present important drawbacks in the treatment of
low-eccentricity orbits. In particular, the formal series representing the perturbation
solution lack the expected convergence rate of a Taylor series expansion in powers
of J2 in the limited time interval in which the perturbation series apply. Neverthe-
less, as it was pointed out in [30], obtaining a solution in a different set of variables
only requires a reformulation based on the vectorial generating function in Delaunay
variables.
    The challenge is accepted in this paper. More specifically, the closed-form
perturbation theory is reformulated in the orbital set of semi-equinoctial vari-
ables, while preserving the purely periodic character of the mean-to-osculating
35 Page 4 of 32                             The Journal of the Astronautical Sciences (2025) 72:35


transformation. The proposed reformulation is new, to our knowledge, and may
serve as a guide for DSST developers to obtain the long-awaited second-order
analytical J2-model in closed-form truly consistent with the Draper theory.
   The semi-equinoctial variables replace the mean anomaly, the argument of the
periapsis, and the eccentricity, by the mean distance to the node and the projec-
tions of the eccentricity vector in the nodal frame. To make an orbital set, they
are complemented with the right ascension of the ascending node, the third com-
ponent of the angular momentum vector, and the Delaunay action [31]. The cus-
tomary choice of replacing the two later by the inclination and the semimajor
axis is here disregarded in order to keep the reduced dimension of the differential
system.
   The obtained results are totally satisfactory, providing the expected accuracy of
a second-order perturbation theory of this kind. The singularity of the semi-equi-
noctial variables for equatorial orbits, in which case the node is not defined, is not
of worry. The nature of this singularity is not geometric and can be avoided by a
rotation of the reference frame, or by a change to variables specifically adapted to
this case [32, 33]. In the end, an orbit propagator suite commonly relies on dif-
ferent perturbation theories. Undeniably, reformulating the theory in Poincaré’s
nonsingular variables or in any of the variants of the set of equinoctial variables
will solve the issue. However, this case is not tackled and the assignment is left
open. Those interested, if any, should be able to replicate the steps described in
this paper by themselves in order to achieve an analytical mean-to-osculating
transformation that is purely periodic in the fast Poincaré or equinoctial variable.
   The rest of the paper is organized as follows. The formulation of the J2-prob-
lem differential system in the Delaunay variables is recalled in Sect. 2, where
the variations of the semi-equinoctial elements are also displayed to check that
they are free from geometric singularities. The steps needed in the reformula-
tion of the closed-form solution of [29, 30] in the semi-equinoctial variables are
detailed in Sect. 3. A way of refining the second-order solution without need of
computing the third-order terms of the vectorial generator is described in Sect. 4.
These higher-order refinements may be considered superfluous for a real per-
turbation problem due to the variety of effects that must be taken into account
and are missed by the J2-problem. However, their computation clearly illustrates
the philosophy of the perturbation method as well as the fundamental need of a
proper initialization of the mean elements, so many times ignored. Finally, illus-
trative tests of the performance of the new solution are provided in Sect. 5. For
real orbits, the examples chosen would undergo clearly observable effects due to
the atmospheric drag, which is not included in the model. However, this is not the
point, as far as they illustrate the performance of the perturbation theory, and per-
mitted us to compare it with previously reported tests in the literature.
   For completeness, the basic equations of the Lie transforms method for vecto-
rial flows are summarized in Appendix A. Yet, some acquaintance of the reader
with the method is assumed, who is referred to the pertinent literature for details.
For the same reason, needed equations from the literature are provided in Appen-
dix B.

5 Performance Tests

To illustrate that the noncanonical perturbation solution provides the expected per-
formance, two different examples are presented. The first one is a low-eccentricity
orbit, which justifies the efforts in constructing a theory in nonsingular variables. As
mentioned before, the theory based on Delaunay variables would not be adequate for
propagating semi-analytically this kind of orbit due to the appearance of the eccen-
tricity in denominators of the mean-to-osculating transformation, with the conse-
quent lack of convergence. The other example is a Geosynchronous transfer orbit
(GTO), whose eccentric is very high and is the type of orbit that justifies the efforts
in obtaining the closed form of the perturbation theory. In all cases the gravitational
potential is defined through the values 𝜇 = 398600.4415 km3 ∕s2, R⊕ = 6378.1363
km, J2 = 1.082634 × 10−3.
    The true, reference orbits used in the comparisons were computed from corre-
sponding Runge-Kutta integrations of the original differential equations in Cartesian
coordinates. To guarantee the quality of the true solutions, it has been checked that
the different numerical propagations conserved the integrals of the J2-problem—
namely, the energy and the third component of the angular momentum—with at
least 14 digits. Maintaining these high-accuracy standards for tests involving long-
time intervals, say longer than one week, required to carry out the Runge-Kutta inte-
grations in extended precision. On the other hand, the accuracy provided by standard
numerical integration routines of popular symbolic processors was found enough to
investigate the performance of the semianalytical solution in shorter time intervals.

6 Conclusion

The derivation of the mean-to-osculating transformation from a generating function
is a distinctive feature of the method of Lie transforms. It allowed us to obtain a
second-order perturbation solution to the J2-problem in closed form and arbitrary
variables that exactly separates short- and long-period terms. Particularization of
the mean-to-osculating transformation for a preferred set of variables only requires
fixing the arbitrary integration functions of the slow variables, or “constants,” on
which this generator depends upon. Our approach takes advantage of the simplic-
ity provided by the set of Delaunay variables in the construction of the perturbation
solution. Relying on the traditional set of Keplerian orbital elements, which enjoy
analogous characteristics, would have been an acceptable alternative option.
   The procedure has been illustrated with the reformulation of the perturbation
solution in the set of nonsingular semi-equinoctial elements. To do that it has been
imposed that the integration constants of the vectorial generator yield a mean-to-
osculating transformation of the semi-equinoctial elements that is purely periodic in
the mean distance to the ascending node. An equivalent constraint could have been
imposed to a different set of variables, as, for instance, the popular set of equinoctial
variables.

mean that the used theory is flawed either in its conception or in its software

expected from the perturbation solution. But this important issue is not tackled in
the paper, where checking that the optimizations provided by the software com-

than satisfactory result.
